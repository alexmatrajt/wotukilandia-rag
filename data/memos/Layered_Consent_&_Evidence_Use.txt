SUBJECT: Use of Layered Consent and Memory Echo Evidence in Cognitive Investigations

---

SECTION: BACKGROUND

This memorandum analyzes whether a cognitive investigation involving partial layered consent and degraded memory echoes can support enforcement action under Zephyrian law.

The inquiry arises from a scenario in which a coordinating entity obtained surface-layer consent from multiple participants and subsequently conducted deeper resonance mapping without obtaining additional authorization.

The investigation also relied on residual memory echoes that exhibited moderate degradation and potential temporal distortion.

---

SECTION: QUESTIONS PRESENTED

1. Whether surface-layer consent permits access to deeper cognitive layers.
2. Whether degraded memory echoes may be used as reliable evidence.
3. What level of authorization is required for deep memory extraction.

---

SECTION: SHORT ANSWER

Surface-layer consent does not authorize deeper cognitive access.

Degraded memory echoes are insufficient as primary evidence unless verified for reliability.

Deep memory extraction requires either explicit core-layer consent or a valid Tri-Mind Warrant.

---

SECTION: ANALYSIS

Subsection A: Layered Consent Requirements

The Telepathic Data Protection Act (Version 2.0), Article 3.1, requires that consent correspond to the depth of cognitive access.

In Zephyria v. Lume Collective (Cycle 3024), the Supreme Court clarified that layered consent operates as distinct authorization levels rather than a generalized permission structure.

Accordingly, consent limited to surface signals cannot justify access to core-layer memories.

Additionally, the Cognitive Signal Handling Regulations, Article 3.1, reinforce that consent must match signal classification levels.

Subsection B: Deep Memory Extraction

Article 4.1 of the Telepathic Data Protection Act prohibits deep memory extraction absent a Tri-Mind Warrant.

The absence of such authorization renders any extracted core-layer data unlawfully obtained.

Even where investigative urgency exists, the statutory framework does not permit substitution of partial consent for formal authorization.

Subsection C: Use of Memory Echoes

Article 8.1 of the Telepathic Data Protection Act restricts the use of retroactive memory echoes unless verified for temporal distortion.

In In re Xel’thar Incident Review (Cycle 3025), the Supreme Court held that degraded memory echoes lacking verification cannot serve as primary evidence.

Further, the Cognitive Signal Handling Regulations, Article 4.2, require that residual echoes be assigned a degradation index.

Where such indexing is absent or indicates unreliability, evidentiary use is significantly limited.

Subsection D: Risk Considerations

Reliance on improperly obtained cognitive data may result in:
(a) exclusion of evidence;
(b) enforcement invalidation;
(c) sanctions by the Cognitive Integrity Council.

---

SECTION: CONCLUSION

The legal framework governing cognitive investigations in Zephyria imposes strict alignment between consent and depth of access.

Surface-level consent is insufficient to justify deeper cognitive intrusion.

Additionally, degraded memory echoes cannot be relied upon without verification of integrity.

Any enforcement action based on such evidence is likely to face significant legal challenge and potential invalidation.

---

SECTION: RECOMMENDATIONS

1. Obtain explicit layered consent corresponding to intended depth of access.
2. Secure a Tri-Mind Warrant for any core-layer memory extraction.
3. Verify all memory echoes for degradation and temporal distortion prior to use.
4. Maintain full compliance logs to support evidentiary integrity.
